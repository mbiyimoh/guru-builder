## **OPENING PHASE MODULES**

### **Module 1: Basic Opening Rolls**

**Position Examples:**
- **Opening 3-1**: Correct play is 8/5, 6/5 (making the 5-point)
- **Opening 6-1**: Correct play is 13/7, 8/7 (making the 7-point)  
- **Opening 4-2**: Correct play is 8/4, 6/4 (making the 4-point)
- **Opening 6-5**: Correct play is 24/13 (running one back checker)

**Exercises:**
1. Memorize the top 3 plays for each opening roll from 1-1 through 6-6
2. Practice quick recognition: Given an opening roll, name the best play within 3 seconds
3. Explain why 3-1: 8/5, 6/5 is better than 3-1: 24/21, 13/12

**Practice Problems:**
- You open with 2-1. What's the best play and why?
- Opponent opens 4-3 playing 24/21, 13/9. You roll 6-1. What do you play?
- You open 5-3. Compare 13/8, 13/10 vs 13/10, 24/21. Which is better?

### **Module 2: Slotting Strategy**

**Position Examples:**
- **Opening 6-2**: Aggressive slot: 24/18, 13/11 vs Safe play: 24/16
- **Opening 2-1**: Bold slot: 13/11, 6/5 vs Conservative: 24/23, 13/11
- **Responding to 6-1 (13/7, 8/7)**: Rolling 5-2, do you play 24/17 or 13/8, 13/11?

**Exercises:**
1. Identify 5 opening rolls where slotting is the top choice
2. Calculate: If you slot the 11-point, what are opponent's chances of hitting next turn?
3. Compare risk/reward of different slotting options for the same roll

**Practice Problems:**
- You roll 5-4 to open. Compare 13/8, 13/9 vs 24/15. When would you choose each?
- After opponent's 3-1 (8/5, 6/5), you roll 4-1. Analyze 24/20, 13/12 vs other plays
- Opening 1-1: Is 8/7(2), 6/5(2) too bold? Compare with safer alternatives

### **Module 3: Splitting and Running**

**Position Examples:**
- **Opening 4-3**: Run with 24/17 vs Split with 24/21, 13/9 vs Build with 13/9, 13/10
- **Opening 6-4**: 24/14 vs 24/18, 13/9 vs 8/2, 6/2
- **Responding to opponent's split**: They play 24/23, 13/11, you roll 6-3

**Exercises:**
1. Calculate how often your split checker gets attacked from the bar-point
2. Practice identifying when early running is correct vs. risky
3. Learn follow-up plays after successful splits

**Practice Problems:**
- You split 24/23 with part of your roll. Opponent makes their 4-point. Now what?
- Compare opening 6-5: 24/13 vs 24/18, 18/13. Which is more flexible?
- After 24/23 split, you're hit and re-enter on 2-point. How do you continue?

### **Module 4: Point-Making Priorities**

**Position Examples:**
- **5-point vs 4-point**: When you can make either, why choose 5-point?
- **7-point timing**: Positions where making bar-point is more urgent than 5-point  
- **Lower points**: When making 3-point or 2-point becomes necessary

**Exercises:**
1. Rank all 24 points by general value in opening/early middle game
2. Identify positions where normal point-making priority changes
3. Practice recognizing "point-making races" between opponents

**Practice Problems:**
- You can make your 3-point or your opponent's 5-point (20-point). Which is better?
- Both players are fighting to make the 5-point. You have builders on 8 and 6. Strategy?
- Mid-game position: You can make 9-point, 10-point, or 11-point. How do you choose?

### **Module 5: Responding to Opponent's Opens**

**Position Examples:**
- **vs Slotting Opens**: Opponent plays 6-2: 24/18, 13/11. You roll 5-4, 4-3, or 3-1

## **MIDDLE GAME MODULES**

### **Module 6: Hitting and Covering**

**Position Examples:**
- **Safe hits**: Enemy blot on your 9-point, you have builders on 11 and 10
- **Risky hits**: Enemy blot, but hitting leaves you very exposed
- **Double hits**: Positions where you can hit two enemy checkers

**Exercises:**
1. Calculate covering chances after hitting with different builder combinations
2. Practice recognizing when NOT to hit (timing, position considerations)
3. Learn to evaluate hit-vs-split decisions in middle game

**Practice Problems:**
- Enemy checker on your 10-point. You can hit from 13 with a 3. Do you hit if you have no builders?
- Double hit opportunity: You can hit on 20 and 22 points. When is this correct?
- You hit enemy checker but can't cover. When is this still the right play?

**Position Examples:**

- **5-point prime**: Constructing and maintaining longer primes
- **6-point prime**: The perfect prison - how to build and advance

**Exercises:**
1. Practice moving primes forward without breaking them
2. Learn optimal checker distribution for prime building
3. Calculate how many rolls needed to escape different prime lengths

**Practice Problems:**
- You have 4-point prime (9-8-7-6). How do you safely advance it?
- Building 5-prime: You have 8-7-6-5, enemy checker on 11. Next moves?
- 6-point prime: You've built 9-8-7-6-5-4. How do you time the advance?

### **Module 9: Back Game Strategy**

**Position Examples:**

**Exercises:**
1. Set up ideal back game positions and practice timing
2. Learn to recognize when back game is failing
3. Practice transitioning from defense to offense in back games

**Practice Problems:**
- Back game with 20,18 anchors: Timing checkers on 23,22,21. Strategy?
- Opponent offers double in your back game position. Take or drop?
- Your back game timing is off - too many checkers. Recovery plan?

### **Module 12: Breaking Contact**

**Position Examples:**
- **Safe break**: Clear pip lead, safe to disengage
- **Risky break**: Close race, contact might favor you
- **Forced break**: Position demands breaking contact despite risks

**Exercises:**
1. Calculate minimum pip lead needed to break contact safely
2. Practice identifying "must break" vs "must stay" positions  
3. Learn gradual disengagement vs. immediate break strategies

**Practice Problems:**
- 8-pip lead in race. Opponent has 20-point anchor. Break contact?
- You're 15 pips behind but have strong attack. Stay or go?

### **Module 13: Timing in Complex Positions**

**Position Examples:**
- **Perfect timing**: Your position develops just as opponent weakens
- **Poor timing**: Must break your position while opponent is strong
- **Timing adjustment**: Moves to improve your timing

**Exercises:**
1. Count moves until position changes (anchor breaks, prime collapses)
2. Practice stalling and acceleration techniques
3. Learn to recognize timing advantages/disadvantages

**Practice Problems:**

### **Module 14: The Race Decision**

**Position Examples:**
- **Clear racing position**: Both sides ready to run, pip count decisive
- **Borderline racing**: Close pip count, position factors matter
- **Anti-racing position**: Behind in pips but position strongly favors contact

**Exercises:**
1. Learn "racing formulas" - pip lead needed to race at different game stages
2. Practice evaluating non-pip factors (home boards, timing, etc.)
3. Compare pure racing vs. maintaining tactical chances

**Practice Problems:**

**Practice Problems:**

## **GENERAL PRACTICE RECOMMENDATIONS**

### **Daily Training Routine:**
- **5 minutes**: Opening roll recognition drill
- **10 minutes**: Position judgment exercises from current learning module
- **10 minutes**: Pip counting practice
- **5 minutes**: Bear-off technique refinement

### **Weekly Assessment:**
- Play through example positions from completed modules
- Take timed tests on opening plays and basic techniques
- Practice transitioning between game phases smoothly
- Review and reinforce weaker areas identified during play

### **Equipment Needed:**
- Physical backgammon board for hands-on practice
- Online training software for automated drills
- Position reference books for additional examples
- Practice partner or computer opponent for gameplay

### **Progression Notes:**
- Master each module before advancing to the next
- Spend extra time on modules that match your preferred playing style
- Return to earlier modules periodically for reinforcement
- Focus on weak areas identified through actual game play

*Remember: Understanding these concepts intellectually is just the beginning. True mastery comes through thousands of repetitions and practical application in actual games.*
